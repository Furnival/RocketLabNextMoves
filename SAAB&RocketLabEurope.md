# Essay —SAAB ahd Rocket Lab Europe -- collaboration?  
## Two NATO-Native Space Companies, One Obvious Conversation  
*An open note to Saab engineers on why Rocket Lab Europe is worth a look*    
Something quiet but important happened in Munich this April. Rocket Lab closed its acquisition of Mynaric, stood up Rocket Lab Europe as a semi-autonomous German entity, and — with the German FDI approval that took thirteen months to earn — became the only NATO-native optical communications terminal manufacturer in Europe outside Airbus/Tesat.  
For engineers building on the Saab side, that changes the map. 

## What Saab does well, and what it doesn’t  
Saab’s strengths are unusually clear: mission systems integration, secure tactical communications, airborne ISR, precision effects, and — since March 2024 — a NATO-native prime contractor identity that opens doors in Berlin, Warsaw, Tallinn, and Helsinki that were previously half-closed. GlobalEye, Gripen E, Giraffe, and the Combitech portfolio give Saab a seat at the table for essentially every European proliferated-LEO conversation happening today.
What Saab does not do: manufacture SDA-grade optical inter-satellite link terminals, launch small satellites on demand, or build satellite buses at proliferated-constellation cadence. Those capabilities have historically meant partnering with American suppliers subject to ITAR, or with Airbus subsidiaries whose incentives run through Toulouse rather than Linköping.  

## What Rocket Lab Europe brings  
Rocket Lab Europe operates one product line that matters immediately to Saab engineers: the CONDOR optical communications terminal, marketed as ITAR-free, manufactured and qualification-tested in Gilching, and already delivering over 100 units to the US Space Development Agency’s Proliferated Warfighter Space Architecture. The Mk3.1 variant now in development targets 100 Gbps — precisely the envelope IRIS² and SATCOMBw Stufe 4 are pointing at.
Beyond terminals, Rocket Lab brings satellite bus heritage (Photon, over 200 spacecraft produced), Electron and forthcoming Neutron launch, and — through Rocket Lab Europe’s structure — the ability to hold European sovereign contracts and accept European sovereign co-investment. This is not a US company with a German mailing address. The German FDI review made sure of that.

## The four places the two companies roadmaps intersect
1. SATCOMBw Stufe 4. The Bundeswehr’s ~€10 billion proliferated LEO tender is where this partnership matters most. Neither of the two competing consortia currently pairs a NATO-native mission-system integrator with a NATO-native optical mesh supplier. Saab plus Rocket Lab Europe as a joint sub-tier team — Saab on battle-management, secure waveforms, and user segment; Rocket Lab Europe on OISL and bus — is the cleanest response to the BMVg 2025 strategy’s explicit requirement for Allied-network interoperability.
2. Airborne optical downlink. Mynaric’s HAWK airborne terminal exists precisely for the mission profile Gripen E and GlobalEye are evolving toward: high-bandwidth sensor data flowing off-platform to a space relay and onward to distributed command nodes. If Saab is thinking about the next-generation datalink for its airborne fleet, an all-optical, ITAR-free, European-manufactured chain is now available.
3. Nordic sovereign space. NORDEFCO conversations about a Nordic proliferated capability are quietly maturing. Sweden, Finland, Norway, and Denmark share a specific problem — heavy dependence on US-controlled space assets — and no coherent industrial answer. Saab as Nordic prime, Rocket Lab Europe as optical and bus supplier, with ICEYE and KSAT rounding out the segment, is the only combination that closes the industrial gap without going through Toulouse.
4. Resilient PNT. GPS jamming across the Baltic has moved alternative PNT from a research topic to a procurement one. Rocket Lab launched ESA’s first LEO-PNT demonstrators this March, and Mynaric’s optical-timing capabilities offer a natural space segment for a Saab-led resilient-PNT integration play across NATO’s eastern flank.
   
## The engineering culture point  
Both companies share an operating tempo that is genuinely unusual in European defense. Rocket Lab has flown 65+ Electron missions, iterated Photon across dozens of customer variants, and moved Mynaric from restructuring to over 100 CONDOR deliveries inside a single procurement cycle. Saab moved GlobalEye from concept to combat-ready in under a decade and has kept the Gripen E development curve tight against far larger competitors.
Neither organization is built around the ten-year system procurement. Both are built around getting hardware into orbit or into the air on schedules that would make legacy primes nervous. Engineers on either side would recognize the other’s rhythm.

## The possibility
There is no announced Saab–Rocket Lab Europe partnership as of this writing. There should be a conversation. If you work on Saab’s space, satellite communications, airborne datalink, or PNT programs and the industrial logic above resonates, the engineering-to-engineering contact is where partnerships like this actually start — long before either company’s press office is involved.  

Munich is a short flight from Linköping, Järfälla, or Stockholm.   

*Views expressed here are the author’s own and reflect publicly disclosed program information as of July 2026. Of course, any actual partnership would proceed through proper business development channels at both companies. Disclosure: the author is a shareholder in both Rocket Lab and SAAB.*
